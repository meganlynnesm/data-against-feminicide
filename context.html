Data Against Feminicide
Overview

Femicide is a global problem that remains chronically undercounted. In 2017 alone, an estimated 87,000 women were intentionally killed worldwide, yet no reliable data exists on how many of these deaths were gender-related — a gap rooted in weak or narrowly framed legislation, inconsistent government reporting, and a media landscape that often fails to cover the murders of Black and Indigenous women as newsworthy. Across the Americas and beyond, activists and civil society organizations have stepped up, producing what researchers term "counterdata": records built outside official statistics that document not just the scale of the problem, but the identities of the women themselves. Projects like Feminicidio Uruguay and the Sovereign Bodies Institute exemplify this work, treating data collection as an act of memory and justice as much as measurement. Data Against Feminicide emerged directly from this landscape and brought together researchers and activist practitioners to understand how this counterdata is produced, and to build tools that support the people doing that work.

Timeline
2015

Helena Suárez Val founds Feminicidio Uruguay, monitoring and mapping femicide cases, growing out of protest work with the Coordinadora de Feminismos del Uruguay.

2016

Helena continues the project independently, publishing ongoing data on feminicides, attempted feminicides, and other violent deaths of women.

2017

ILDA, led by Silvana Fumega, begins an action-research study on femicide data across Latin America and the Caribbean, backed by IDRC and the Avina Foundation, later joined by the IDB.

2019

Catherine D'Ignazio, on sabbatical in Buenos Aires while writing Data Feminism, meets Silvana; Helena begins a PhD at Warwick on femicide data's social effects. In August, Catherine, Helena, and Silvana meet and Data Against Feminicide is founded.

2020

Data Feminism (MIT Press) is published with Lauren F. Klein, framing femicide data gaps as "missing data" and activist data as "feminist counterdata." Catherine establishes the Data + Feminism Lab; Isadora Cruxên joins DCF as a student researcher.

2021–23

Isadora, with Alessandra Jungs de Almeida, leads the co-design and Portuguese translation of DCF's tools with Brazilian activists.

2023

Isadora Cruxên formally joins the DCF leadership team, bringing her interest in political mobilization and the politics of data production.
