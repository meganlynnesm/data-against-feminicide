Methodology

Understand the methodology underlying the practice: software approach, data uses & provenance, theoretical methods.

  • South-North(Latin America and North America) participatory action research and design (see Brief)
  • Activist-produced "counterdata" rather than centrally collected or aggregated data (see the Brief page's closing note)
  • Four activity areas — community, research, tool co-design, training (see Brief)
  • Concrete tools: Data Highlighter, Email Alerts System, Language localization (see Ontology)

DCF's methodology in documenting, analysing, and representing their research emphasizes collaboration, community engagement, and the use of activist-produced data to challenge dominant narratives about feminicide. The project employs a participatory action research approach, which involves working closely with activists and civil society organizations to understand their practices and challenges in producing counterdata. This approach allows for the co-design of tools and resources that support the work of these activists, while also ensuring that their voices and experiences are central to the research process. The project's methodology also emphasizes the importance of transparency and accountability in data collection and analysis, as well as the need for ethical considerations when working with sensitive data related to gender-based violence.

Rhetorical Analysis

What is the precedent trying to say? What are its arguments? How is it "critical"?

  • Visual identity leans toward protest/zine collage rather than institutional polish (see Visual Analysis)
  • Explicit framing: the project supports activists' existing practices rather than collecting data itself (see Brief)
  • Academic framing via "missing data" and "feminist counterdata" (D'Ignazio & Klein, Data Feminism, 2020 — see Context, Bibliography)

DCF makes the argument that data activism can challenge dominant narratives about violence against women and girls, particularly in contexts where such data is systematically absent or marginalized. They draw attention to the importance of supporting activists' existing practices and the need for tools that facilitate data collection, analysis, and dissemination. The project's visual and rhetorical choices reinforce its critical stance, emphasizing grassroots engagement and the lived experiences of those affected by feminicide.

Wider Practice

How does the project fit into the author's wider practice?

  • Catherine D'Ignazio — Data + Feminism Lab (MIT); Data Feminism (2020)
  • Silvana Fumega — ILDA's regional action-research study on femicide data
  • Helena Suárez Val — Feminicidio Uruguay; PhD research at Warwick on femicide data's social effects
  • Isadora Cruxên — data activism & participatory AI research (see Bibliography)

DCF covers Latin America and North America and occasionaly touches on cases within Europe. The founding team's interdisciplinary approach combines technical expertise with community engagement, reflecting a commitment to socially responsible data practices. All members of the founding team have a history of working on issues related to feminicide and gender-based violence, and their broader work informs the project's methodology and objectives. The project serves as a platform for collaboration and knowledge exchange among researchers, activists, and practitioners in the field of data activism against feminicide.

Personal Assessment

Evaluation of Datos Contra Feminicidio

Datos Contra Feminicidio is a significant contribution to the field of data activism, particularly in its focus on challenging dominant narratives about violence against women and girls. The project's emphasis on grassroots engagement and the lived experiences of those affected by feminicide is commendable. I think the project's website is well-designed and effectively communicates its mission and values throuogh use of colour, visuals, and typography. What I thought was particularly effective was the navigation structure and clear layout of the site and the way it presents complex information in an accessible manner.
My criticism lies in the limited representation of diverse voices and perspectives within the project's framework however I recognise that many of the project collaborators bring these to light. While I do mention the clean graphics and visuals, I think the project could benefit from more interactive elements that allow users to engage with the data and stories being presented. I think the last leasson in d3 and data visualisation could be applied to this project to make it more interactive and engaging for users.
Overall, I think the project is a valuable resource for researchers, activists, and anyone interested in understanding and addressing the issue of feminicide in Latin America and beyond.
